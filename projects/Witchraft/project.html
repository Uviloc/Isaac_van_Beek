Process explanation

A multiplayer game about witches racing on hovercrafts through a swamp.
This was my first experience with the Unreal engine with a project lasting 10 weeks in a team of 8 where I had not worked with any of its members before.

Game page:
Witchraft Itch.io

Witchraft was a joint project made during a course at Creative Media & Game Technologies. Made in a group of 8 (2 designers, 4 artist, 2 engineers), the project was made in Unreal Engine 5.2.
This was my first experience with Unreal, so there were many unforeseen problems that I encountered. The project spanned a total of 10 weeks and only a couple of people had experience in Unreal. In the end I would not recommend doing this project in Unreal if it wasn't for all the experience I learned along the way.
The project was our first time working with and for a client. However we did not really notice much of a difference in the way we approached to project. Since despite a lot of trying we were often ignored or given very little feedback by our client.

As is with many group projects there were a lot of issues. Some caused by miscommunication, others caused by simple inexperience in the engine. Such as all the issues that came along with using Git for Unreal.
This caused a lot of delays and we ended up having to scrap certain parts of the original concept. For instance, there was a plan to have the buildings explode on impact.
However the Chaos System (from Unreal) was having too many issues and we decided it to not be worth solving the numerous issues.

Gameplay

Procedural Foliage in Unreal

I was personally responsible for the designing and building of the level.
I used the procedural generator offered by Unreal to plant the vegetation.

Level Design

At first the idea was to create more of a closed loop with a couple of branches. The idea that many in our group wanted was to have it almost be a maze.
I personally do not like confusion as a game aspect, but I do like chaos as a game aspect.
However during the first testing phase we quickly realized that this was just too much for the players to keep track of.
So I simplified the map significantly by having a straight path without incorrect choices.
This, during playtesting, worked much better in terms of guiding the player to the finish.

Volumetric Fog

In the end I learned a lot about Unreal. Mostly in terms of the level editor, but I also made a volumetric fog texture with the materials.
I used Substance Designer textures with its respective plugin and I also fixed some bugs in the blueprints, that the engineers didn't have the time for.

Reflection

An interesting aspect about me is that I do have experience in both designing, coding and a bunch in between.
I only code mostly as a hobby so I would not be able to replace an actual engineer.
I don't have experience in art, but I do always try to understand how things work in their field.
Because of this I am able to give another view and am able to brainstorm with a lot of people in different fields.
I often find solutions and the reasons behind bugs, that the engineers haven't been able to solve.
Like the fact that player 2 would disappear when player 1 moved too far.
I immediately though it could be related to the world partitioning we had in place. And low and behold, it was.

I was also appointed the project leader. This is not something too new to me, as I often end up leading all the projects in my study. I generally have a good and quick grasp on how people work and what they are good with.
And although I have many parts to improve in I always try to resolve problems in the group by looking at the situation from an objective perspective.

This project was quite difficult for me in this role however.
This was due to me only knowing 2 of the members, while I never worked with any of them.
So it was hard to keep the entire group together as many members were getting irritated with the project, the client and others.
It certainly did not help that some of the members were only complaining and borderline insulting others.
Looking back I really should have stopped this behavior from happening, as it would have improved the overall mood in the end.

I spend by far the most hours into this project. Mostly due to me working on a lot of the various tasks that nobody else really took up.
Like testing, communication with the client, prototyping and creating the presentations. Although that last one I liked to do anyways.
Since I have a bunch of experience in PowerPoint, I often go a bit of an extra mile on the presentations, which is why I created almost all presentations during my study.
